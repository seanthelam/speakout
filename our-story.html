Our Story

Owen Blazick

(he/him)

I am a white, cis-man from a small town in Pennsylvania called Mountain Top. I lived in Mountain Top for my entire life; I was born in a small city not too far away from my house, and the northeast Pennsylvania region is the only place I have ever called home. My hometown is predominantly white; it includes various suburbs, most of which are home to middle-class white families. There are some very wealthy neighborhoods, but most of the neighborhoods in my town are more middle-class.

I had very limited exposure to people who did not share the same race as me. For as long as I can remember, my classmates, teammates, and friends were mostly white. I didn’t realize it at the time, but I had the privilege of living a life free of racial discrimination; no one ever made harsh comments about my race. I was part of the majority. I got to enjoy the luxury of “fitting in” as a white person in what I would consider to be a white space. As a member of a white upper middle-class family in a mostly white town, I had many opportunities that others did not. I got to enjoy expensive sports like golf and skiing. I was a member of a community at a country club that showed me tremendous amounts of support throughout my childhood. As a child, I never really had to think about my racial identity since I “belonged”.

As I progressed through school, I started to recognize racial inequality. I began to notice racist tendencies in and out of my school environment around the time I entered high school. Some of my classmates would make racist remarks or jokes, and everyone would laugh at them. These types of comments rarely received any kind of backlash; they were made by white people and received by white people. Similar situations would happen at the country club. Some members would make racist comments, and no one would bat an eye; these comments were considered normal in this setting.

Looking back at my childhood, I’ve become aware of the fact that I grew up in a very conservative environment. I think this is the reason I started to notice racism during my high school years. During Donald Trump’s presidency, many residents of my town felt that their racist beliefs were justified to some extent, so they felt more comfortable expressing their beliefs. I believe the insulated nature of the white space I lived in promoted the exchange of racist comments, some more explicit than others. As a member of a liberal household, my father would always complain about the racist nature of our town. My father was one of the main reasons for why I was able to recognize the presence of racism in my town. He made an effort to educate me about things happening outside the white bubble I lived in, and I am extremely grateful for this. As a member of a white space, I understand that growing up in a household that is anti-racist is somewhat of a privilege. I understand that not everyone who comes from a white space is aware of the different aspects of race and racial inequality, and I hope this website succeeds in providing valuable experiences real people have dealt with involving race.

The purpose of this project is to provide a space where we can educate each other about race and racial inequality. When I came to Colorado College, I had little exposure to racial inequality. My experience in block 4 with the class Racial Inequality was very valuable to me, as I learned about the parts of racism I never experienced before. I understand not all Colorado College students will have the opportunity to take this class, so hopefully this website can communicate some of the most important parts of this class.

Sean Lam

(he/him)

I am a first-generation Vietnamese American from a low-income household. I was born in Naples, Florida, but primarily raised in Las Vegas, Nevada and Westminster, California. My father and his family were refugees, who escaped after the Vietnam War and immigrated to the United States. My stepmother immigrated to the United States when she was much older. Both my parents did not have a college education. While we were growing up, my father worked in nail salons until he switched to IT Support. We moved around a lot, and I went to three different elementary schools. They were 90%, 77%, and 95% minority enrollment elementary schools, with primarily Hispanic/Latinx and AAPI students. My middle school was also a 95% minority enrollment school. However, I moved to Colorado Springs in 2019, where my high school was 61% White and 39% minority enrollment. Now, I go to Colorado College, where it is a predominantly White institution. I feel that I have had much exposure to people who share the same race as me. But, it was definitely difficult during my high school and, so far, college experience because there are not many people similar to me. I remember when my family and I would stay inside during most of the COVID-19 pandemic. When it died down, we went out and received racial slurs and anti-Asian hate comments. Even at Colorado College, I have received racial slurs several times while walking across the street. I learned to deal with it and stay quiet, which is probably something that is not a good thing to do. In taking “Racial Inequality,” I have noticed many of my experiences and feelings are relatable to those taught in class. I hope to discuss race and racial inequality more in educational settings. I see a lot of these topics discussed in social media (i.e. Instagram posts), however, I want to create a space through Speak Out for others to be vulnerable, educate others, and explain their experiences.

Jimmy O'Connell

(he/him)

I am a white, cis-man born and raised in San Francisco, California. I have attended PWIs throughout my education, never really reflecting on the white, privileged spaces I grew up in. My middle school, Sts. Peter and Paul, high school, Lick-Wilmerding, and now college, Colorado College, are all majority white. My middle school used to celebrate the “Columbus Day Bazaar” every year, a big festival that the local community took part in. They have since changed it to “Indigenous People’s Day.” My high school was a little better, holding events like “Carnival” to celebrate Hispanic culture, but it was still 62% white. Taking the course “Racial Inequality” at Colorado College, taught by Professor Inara Rodis, has expanded my awareness of the systemic and institutionalized racism that is everywhere in education. Topics about race and racial inequality were rarely discussed in these educational settings, and even within my own family, and I’m willing to bet that a lot of people have the same experience here at CC. So we thought it was a good idea to create a space where students can learn from each others’ experiences regarding racism and racial inequality and start educating themselves about racial inequality in education.
